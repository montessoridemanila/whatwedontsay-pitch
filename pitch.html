01  —  Vision

Most of it goes unspoken.

Students do not always say what they are experiencing, especially in school settings where they are expected to remain composed and continue functioning normally.

This project is designed to give students a safe, structured way to share concerns that are often left unspoken. It will use an anonymous survey organized around six emotional themes, then present selected responses in a curated website written and built by students.

The purpose is not only expression, but documentation: to identify recurring concerns, show how students describe them in their own words, and create a school-based record that can support future wellness efforts.

"A documented record of what students carry — written in their own words, preserved beyond a single school year."

School wellness initiatives are often temporary. Posters are taken down, and seminars conclude after a set period. A published website offers a more durable output — one that can be accessed, shared, and revisited by future students. It serves as a documented record of student experience at Montessori de Manila, based on accounts written by the students themselves.

The project is led by the MDM Secondary Student Council, with welfare oversight from the guidance counselor at every stage. The survey will run in May, editorial work will take place in June, and the website will launch in July for the full Grades 7–12 student body.

Reference

The structural reference for this project is Inside the Head by Chloé Fleury (2019), an anonymous submission-based website organized into visual chapters. We are adapting the format for Montessori de Manila, while keeping the content, visual identity, and editorial voice original.

02  —  Website Chapters

Six themes.
One collective portrait.

The website will function as a scroll-based anthology. Each chapter begins with a full-screen visual title and expands into curated student writing. The six themes were selected because they reflect recurring concerns students raise in school contexts, including academic pressure, family expectations, identity, uncertainty, and social isolation.

Blue — reaching toward
Red — what pushes back
Ch. 01 Pressure

"I was okay with my grades until I heard what everyone else got."

This chapter focuses on academic pressure and performance expectations. Many students experience school as a constant comparison between what is required and what they feel they must prove. Sustained pressure affects concentration, sleep, confidence, and willingness to ask for help.

Ch. 02 Home

"They don't say anything when I mess up, but I can tell they expected better."

This chapter addresses tension between family expectations and personal identity. Some students carry guilt, responsibility, or fear of disappointing the people closest to them — pressure that affects emotional stability, participation, and self-expression in school.

Ch. 03 Longing

"I typed it out three times and still didn't send it."

This chapter examines private emotional life, including attachment, unspoken feelings, and the difficulty of naming what a student feels toward another person. Students often keep these experiences hidden, even when they shape daily behavior and relationships.

Ch. 04 After

"Everyone keeps asking what course I'm taking. I don't even know what I'm good at."

This chapter addresses uncertainty about the future. For many students, the concern is not only exam performance, but the pressure to know what comes next before they feel ready. This section documents anxiety about direction, identity, and expectations after graduation.

Ch. 05 Self

"I act different depending on who I'm with, and I don't know which one people think is me."

This chapter explores identity under repetition. Students are often described by labels formed early in school life, even after they have changed. School environments can either allow growth or keep students fixed inside outdated perceptions.

Ch. 06 Alone

"I'm always with people, but I still feel like I don't belong anywhere."

This chapter addresses emotional invisibility and social isolation in familiar environments. A student can be physically present, socially active, and still feel unseen. Loneliness in school is often quiet, but it has real effects on belonging, participation, and well-being.

03  —  The Survey

Anonymous by design.
Clear by purpose.

The survey begins with a short explanation of the project and a consent checkbox. It then moves through six sections, one for each chapter. Each section contains five to ten questions, combining open-ended responses with optional scale items. The full form will contain approximately 30 to 60 questions and will be distributed through Google Forms during a free period so students can respond at their own pace.

Form Architecture

How it works

The survey is designed to collect meaningful student responses without forcing them into one way of answering. Each chapter begins with a short framing line that identifies the topic while still leaving room for personal interpretation. Open-ended responses will remain the primary format.

The survey will not collect names, email addresses, or account sign-ins. It is designed to be appropriate for Grades 7–12, with wording reviewed for clarity, emotional safety, and age accessibility. A safe-space note, prepared with the guidance counselor, will appear at the top and bottom of the form.

Sample Questions — Per Chapter

What we're asking

  • Pressure — "What part of school stresses you out the most during a normal week?"
  • Home — "What expectation from home affects you the most as a student?"
  • Longing — "What is something you wish you could say to someone but haven't?"
  • After — "What about your future feels most uncertain to you right now?"
  • Self — "What part of yourself do you feel people at school don't really see?"
  • Alone — "When do you feel most disconnected from other people at school?"
Optional Quantitative Layer

Scale & multiple-choice items

  • How often do you feel stressed because of schoolwork? (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Always)
  • How much pressure do you feel from your family regarding school? (None / Low / Moderate / High / Very High)
  • How often do you hold back what you want to say to someone? (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Always)
  • How confident are you about your future plans? (Not at all / Slightly / Moderately / Very / Extremely)
  • Do you feel labeled or judged based on past behavior? (Yes / Sometimes / No)
  • How often do you feel alone at school? (Never / Rarely / Sometimes / Often / Always)
Ethics Protocol

Consent, anonymity, welfare

  • No names or email addresses will be collected. Only grade level information will be recorded.
  • An explicit consent checkbox is required before answering the survey.
  • The guidance counselor reviews all questions before release.
  • The guidance counselor will monitor responses for any signs of distress or need for intervention.
  • Any response that could identify a student will not be published.
  • The admin will receive a full website preview before launch.

04  —  Website Design

A website that supports
readability, clarity, and accessibility.

White will serve as the dominant background to keep the site open and readable. Navy will function as the structural color. Blue will mark chapters associated with movement, reflection, or reaching forward. Red will mark chapters associated with pressure, strain, or conflict.

Aa Typography

Playfair Display + Instrument Sans

Playfair Display will be used for chapter titles and pull quotes. Instrument Sans will be used for labels, captions, navigation, and body copy. The contrast is intentional — one typeface gives the site weight and presence, the other supports clarity and readability.

Il. Illustration

Flat, abstract, faceless figures

Each chapter will include one full-bleed illustration built from flat, abstract, faceless figures. The illustrations are meant to express the emotional tone of each chapter without identifying or personalizing any student.

Pg. Chapter Pages

Student voice as the primary text

Student writing remains the central content. Selected quotations will appear in varied layouts so the pages feel distinct without becoming visually cluttered. Aggregated survey data will be presented typographically rather than through conventional charts.

Rd. Red as Signal

Earns its place every time

Red will be used only where emphasis is necessary — the line that carries the most weight, the figure that changes the meaning of the page. Its use will remain limited to preserve clarity and avoid visual overload.

07  —  Timeline

From survey to publication.

The timeline is built around a realistic approval process and a small student team producing the project from start to finish.

May 1–9

Preparation

Survey questions are finalized, with 5–10 questions per chapter (30–60 total). The Google Form is built and tested. Promotional materials are designed, and the proposal letter is completed in preparation for the May 9 meeting.

May 9
Milestone

Admin Proposal Meeting

The full proposal letter is submitted after the SSC Online Meeting. A separate endorsement letter from the guidance counselor is also submitted. The target is to secure approval within the week so the survey and promotional rollout can proceed before the end of May.

May 12–16
(est.)

Final Preparations, Pending Approval

While awaiting approval, final adjustments are made. The Google Form is reviewed and locked, pubmats are finalized, and the rollout plan is coordinated with homeroom teachers.

May 16
Milestone

Survey + Pubmat Launch

Promotional materials are released through school social media and posted on campus. The survey is distributed through the SSC Instagram account and Google Chat Spaces. A reminder will be sent midway through the collection period.

May 16–30

Collection Window — Two Weeks

The survey remains open for approximately two weeks. The guidance counselor monitors responses throughout this period. The form closes around May 30, and all responses are immediately exported for review.

June 1–20

Editorial Curation

All responses are reviewed by SSC and Montessori Scribes. SSC selects responses for potential inclusion (Pass 1). The Scribes advisor removes or edits any content that could identify a student (Pass 2). Chapter narratives are written and quantitative data is compiled.

June 20 –
July 10

Website Build + Illustrations

Final illustrations for all six chapters are completed. The website is built and all content is integrated. A full review is conducted by SSC, the Scribes advisor, and a faculty partner. An admin preview link is sent at least one week before launch.

Est. mid-July
Milestone

Public Launch

The website is published after receiving admin approval. It is announced through school channels, SSC social media, and Montessori Scribes platforms. QR posters are placed around campus for easy access. The site is made available to all JHS and SHS students.

09  —  Admin Pitch

What to say.
How to say it.

Context

This section is written for delivery in a scheduled meeting. Read it as a school project with a defined purpose — not as an artistic concept alone.

We are proposing a student literary and mental health initiative called What We Don't Say. The project will begin with an anonymous survey organized around six emotional themes. Responses will be collected through Google Forms in May, curated and edited in June, and published as a website in July.

The purpose is to document recurring student concerns in a form that is structured, accessible, and appropriate for a school setting. The final website will present student writing alongside carefully selected quantitative summaries, giving the school a clearer view of what students are carrying and how those concerns appear across the student body.

The project model is Inside the Head by Chloé Fleury (2019), an anonymous submission-based website organized into visual chapters. We are adapting the structure, not the content. Our illustrations, editorial voice, and final material will be original to Montessori de Manila.

What we're asking for: approval to distribute the survey in May, with the guidance counselor involved throughout, and approval to publish the website in July after a full preview has been submitted for review. The guidance counselor has reviewed the survey direction and is formally supporting the project.

10  —  Risk & Ethics

We've thought about
what can go wrong.

Risk

Mental Health Trigger Risk

Questions about anxiety, loneliness, and family expectations may surface genuine distress. The guidance counselor will maintain read-only access during collection and may initiate follow-up if a response suggests a student is in crisis. A safe-space note will appear at the top and bottom of the form.

Risk

Identifiability Risk

Even anonymous responses can become identifiable if the detail is too specific. The editorial review process exists to prevent this. If a response could reasonably reveal who wrote it, it will not be published.

Risk

Low Participation

If response volume is low, the chapters will not feel complete. To reduce that risk, the rollout will include publication material distribution, a mid-window reminder, and peer promotion through SSC officers.

Mitigation

Approval Risk

The proposal will be submitted early enough for review without pressure. A full preview will be shown before publication, and no public launch will happen without sign-off.

Mitigation

Content Quality Control

All content goes through SSC review, Scribes advisor review, and admin preview. Nothing is published until the full chain is complete.

Mitigation

Long-term Scalability

The structure is designed to last beyond one school year. Future SSC teams can add new chapters or build on the archive, turning the site into a record that grows with the school.
